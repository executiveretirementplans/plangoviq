Risk Analysis

ERISA Risk = Transaction Risk

For private equity firms, retirement plan compliance isn't merely an operational concern — it's a direct transaction risk that can derail deals and erode enterprise value.

ERISA violations create hidden liabilities that surface during due diligence, forcing corrective actions that delay closings, trigger purchase price adjustments, or worse, cause deals to collapse entirely. Five critical risk areas threaten transaction certainty and valuation integrity.

Detailed Explanation

When employee 401(k) contributions are not deposited into plan accounts promptly, it constitutes a prohibited transaction under ERISA. The Department of Labor requires deferrals to be deposited as soon as they can reasonably be segregated from company assets — typically within 2-7 business days. Delays beyond this window trigger automatic fiduciary breach liability, interest calculations, and potential civil penalties.

Financial Impact

Typical exposure ranges from $50,000 to $500,000 per occurrence, including lost earnings restoration, DOL penalties, and correction program fees. Repeated violations can trigger formal investigations and multiply penalties.

Case Study: Real-World Impact

Scenario
A manufacturing portfolio company with 450 employees consistently deposited deferrals 14-21 days late due to payroll system integration issues.
Outcome
During buyer due diligence, the issue surfaced in a VCP filing requirement worth $180,000 in lost earnings, plus $75,000 in professional fees. The purchase price was adjusted by $325,000 to account for the unreported liability.
Key Lesson
Late deferral issues discovered during due diligence create immediate valuation drag and delay closing timelines by 30-60 days while corrections are processed.
Detailed Explanation

Misclassifying employees as ineligible for plan participation — due to incorrect age/service calculations, misunderstanding temporary/seasonal worker rules, or failing to track eligibility after status changes — creates significant exposure. Affected employees must be made whole through retroactive employer contributions, including match and profit sharing they should have received, plus lost earnings.

Financial Impact

Correction costs typically range from $100,000 to $2 million depending on the number of affected participants and duration of exclusion. Class action lawsuits from systematically excluded groups can exceed $5 million in settlements.

Case Study: Real-World Impact

Scenario
A healthcare services company incorrectly excluded part-time employees who worked over 1,000 hours annually, affecting approximately 85 employees over a four-year period.
Outcome
The company faced $450,000 in retroactive employer contributions, $120,000 in lost earnings, and $200,000 in legal and actuarial fees to quantify and correct the failure through the IRS correction program.
Key Lesson
Systematic eligibility errors compound over time and create exponentially larger correction costs. Real-time eligibility monitoring prevents multi-year accumulation of liability.
Detailed Explanation

Under ERISA and the Internal Revenue Code, companies under common control are treated as a single employer for retirement plan purposes. Failing to identify controlled group relationships — whether through common ownership, parent-subsidiary structures, or brother-sister affiliations — results in incorrect coverage testing, contribution limit failures, and potentially disqualified plans. This issue frequently surfaces in PE portfolios with multiple related entities.

Financial Impact

Correction costs range from $200,000 to over $3 million, depending on the complexity of the controlled group structure and the number of plans involved. Failed coverage testing can require retroactive contributions to non-highly compensated employees across all related entities.

Case Study: Real-World Impact

Scenario
A PE firm acquired three related companies but failed to recognize they formed a brother-sister controlled group due to overlapping ownership structures. Each company operated separate 401(k) plans with different eligibility rules.
Outcome
Coverage testing failures required $850,000 in retroactive employer contributions to bring excluded employees into compliance. Additionally, the firm faced $400,000 in professional fees and a 12-month delay in plan consolidation initiatives.
Key Lesson
PE acquisitions trigger immediate controlled group analysis requirements. Proactive identification during due diligence prevents post-close surprises and enables strategic plan design.
Detailed Explanation

The IRS requires retirement plans to be amended for legislative changes, regulatory updates, and operational modifications within specific deadlines. Missing required amendments — such as SECURE Act provisions, CARES Act distributions, or annual IRS-required language updates — places the plan's tax-qualified status at risk. Disqualification would trigger immediate taxation of all plan benefits and loss of employer deduction for contributions.

Financial Impact

While plan disqualification is rare, the risk exposure is catastrophic — potentially tens of millions in immediate tax liability across all participants. More commonly, missing amendments discovered during audits result in $50,000-$300,000 in legal fees, document restatement costs, and VCP filing fees.

Case Study: Real-World Impact

Scenario
A technology portfolio company failed to adopt required SECURE Act amendments by the 2022 deadline and continued operating under outdated plan provisions for hardship distributions and required minimum distributions.
Outcome
During a routine IRS audit, the missing amendments were identified. The company paid $95,000 in legal fees to prepare a VCP submission, $25,000 in IRS user fees, and implemented $140,000 in corrective distributions to affected participants.
Key Lesson
Systematic amendment tracking and quarterly compliance calendars ensure legislative changes are adopted timely. Document service providers should be contractually obligated to deliver required amendments automatically.
Detailed Explanation

Plan fiduciaries have a legal duty to ensure retirement plan fees are reasonable relative to services provided. Failure to conduct regular fee benchmarking, document investment due diligence, or negotiate fee reductions as plan assets grow creates litigation vulnerability. The plaintiff's bar actively targets plans with above-median fees, revenue sharing arrangements, or inadequate fiduciary documentation.

Financial Impact

Excessive fee lawsuits typically settle for $1 million to $15 million, with defense costs adding another $500,000 to $3 million. Even successful defenses cost $750,000+ in legal fees and create multi-year management distraction.

Case Study: Real-World Impact

Scenario
A mid-sized portfolio company with a $45 million 401(k) plan had never conducted formal fee benchmarking. Total plan costs were 1.35% annually — approximately 45 basis points above comparable plans — costing participants approximately $200,000 per year in excess fees.
Outcome
Three years after acquisition, participants filed an ERISA lawsuit alleging fiduciary breach. The case settled for $2.4 million, representing three years of alleged excess fees plus prejudgment interest. Defense costs totaled an additional $650,000.
Key Lesson
Annual fee benchmarking with documented fiduciary analysis costs $15,000-$25,000 and provides strong litigation defense. The cost-benefit ratio is overwhelmingly favorable compared to potential exposure.

Proactive Risk Management: Each of these risk factors is preventable through systematic governance, regular compliance audits, and centralized oversight. The cost of prevention is measured in thousands; the cost of correction is measured in hundreds of thousands or millions.
